## CMO POST — LinkedIn (Isaac Donnelson Personal Brand)

Most AI transformation initiatives fail before they start. Not because the technology doesn't work — because the scope is wrong.

We've watched companies run pilots, get leadership excited, then spend six months trying to "scale it across the organization" while the original pilot collects dust. The pattern is consistent enough that it's basically a law at this point.

What actually works:

→ Start with operations, not customer-facing workflows. Internal processes are low-risk. Get them right before automation touches a customer.

→ The three-question test: Does it follow the same steps every time? Does it move data between systems? Does it involve routing, approvals, or notifications? Three yeses = automate it.

→ One workflow, proven, then the next. Teams that try to automate eight things simultaneously end up with eight half-finished workflows nobody trusts.

→ Measure cycle time from day one. Not "number of workflows built" — actual before/after cycle time. That's the business case for the next workflow.

→ The platform decision matters more than most teams realize. A chain of Zapier integrations that breaks when one API changes isn't transformation — it's fragile automation.

## CMO POST — Medium (Isaac Donnelson Personal Brand)

The first question I ask when a company tells me their AI transformation initiative is stalled is always the same: what did you automate first?

The answer is almost always something customer-facing. A chatbot. Automated outreach. An AI-generated email sequence. Something with "AI" in the name that leadership could point to and feel like they were moving fast.

That's where it goes wrong.

Customer-facing workflows carry the highest stakes and require the most judgment. When they misfire — and they do — the consequence is visible to customers. Meanwhile, the operations team is still spending Tuesday mornings manually pulling data from five systems to assemble a report that nobody reads until Wednesday. The finance team is processing invoices by hand at $12–$30 per invoice. The HR coordinator is chasing IT for a new hire's laptop access three days after the offer was accepted.

These are the workflows that should go first. They're rule-based, they're high-frequency, and they're entirely internal — which means when you're testing and iterating, the blast radius of a misconfiguration is an inconvenient morning, not a brand problem.

I built WorkflowFiesta because I kept watching smart teams make this sequencing mistake. They'd buy the platform, build the flashy thing, hit a friction point, and quietly deprioritize the whole initiative. The underlying problem wasn't the technology. It was that nobody had mapped which workflows were actually automatable before they started building.

The 3-question test we use: Does this workflow follow the same steps every time? Does it move data between systems? Does it involve routing, approvals, or notifications? Three yeses means it's a strong automation candidate. One yes means it probably needs a human in the loop.
